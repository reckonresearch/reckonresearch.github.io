1.0

The intelligence that allocates computation

The world spends its largest capital line on silicon, and workloads land on it by default, habit, and vendor lock. Compute is traded as a commodity, dollars per GPU-hour, but consumed as a service: useful work under latency, cost, and energy constraints. The same workload can cost multiples more in one placement than another while meeting the same service target. Frontier labs train models. Providers rent chips. No one is building the system that decides where computation should run.

We build the allocator: a learned world model of computational performance, and the policies that act on it. Three assets make this tractable, and they reinforce each other. The corpus is workload-conditional performance data across silicon, software stacks, providers, prices, and time; hyperscalers hold fragments they cannot publish, and a neutral lab is the only actor that can assemble it with open provenance. The environment is the live compute market, where every placement and migration produces realized costs, met or missed targets, and counterfactuals. The verifier is deterministic ground truth: did this placement meet its p99 at this cost on this silicon. Our verification apparatus is built before our claims are, and learned components earn trust only against it.

Frontier labs make intelligence. We build the intelligence that decides where intelligence runs. Reckon1 is a measurement lab.

1 reckon (/ˈrɛkən/, verb): to compute a quantity; to settle an account; to fix one's position and navigate by it when no landmark is in sight. Three meanings, one discipline: measure, price, and steer the world's computation.

2.0

Why placement

AI has advanced fastest where data is abundant and results are verifiable: math, code, games. Compute placement is such a domain. Every workload run is a labeled measurement, every service target a checkable reward, every dollar of realized savings a ground truth that settles the question. The physics is auditable and the market is live. Here, the machine itself is the environment.

3.0

Research agenda

Now

The calibrated foundation. Auditable analytical models, rooflines and queueing tails, fitted from measured traces; and the placement-premium index, workload-conditional cost differences across silicon and providers, published with confidence intervals and raw data.

Near

Performance surrogates. The open problem: learned models that predict latency, cost, and energy for unseen combinations of workload, silicon, and stack, with uncertainty honest enough to defer to measurement. Generalization across architectures and sim-to-real transfer for performance are unsolved, publishable, and useful to anyone who serves models.

Mid

Allocation policies. Decision policies trained in corpus-calibrated simulation against the verifier, deployed first in shadow, that place, size, and migrate under uncertainty better than static rules.

Horizon

Allocation as a market. Matching and mechanism design for heterogeneous compute where participants are agents, and assay and settlement research for financialized compute markets.

4.0

berth

Placement primitives for inference engineers

Our first instrument. Profile a workload, estimate it across every chip and provider, place and migrate it under a policy you write in plain Python. Apache-2.0, and the core is standard-library only, so every estimate is auditable by hand.

What we publish follows from the same discipline: every number ships with its raw traces, methodology and validators are open, and disputes that arrive with traces attached outrank everything else. Unfavorable results publish. Hardware access never buys editorial input; vendor data is labeled and independently replicated before it enters any published cell.
